## The Fundamentals of Compensation: How to Calculate Pay Raise

Before diving into the formulas, let us establish what constitutes a pay raise. At its simplest, a pay raise is an increase in the rate of compensation an employee receives for their labor. This increase can come in various structures:
- **Salaried adjustments:** A flat annual boost (e.g., adding $5,000 to your yearly base salary).
- **Hourly wage increases:** An increase in your rate per hour (e.g., moving from $25.00/hr to $27.50/hr).
- **Percentage-based adjustments:** An increase defined as a proportion of your current pay (e.g., a 4.5% annual increase).
- **Merit pool increases:** A raise linked to your performance rating (such as a 5-star scale).
- **Federal schedule increases:** Locality adjustments or general schedule step raises.

So, **how do you calculate a pay raise**? The calculation depends on whether you are working with flat rate differences or percentages. Regardless of your pay frequency, the fundamental goal is to convert the adjustment into a common denominator—typically your annual salary or your hourly rate—so you can make an accurate comparison.

## How to Calculate Pay Raise Percentage

The most common way employers express a salary increase is as a percentage. This keeps raises proportional across different pay grades within an organization. 

If you have been offered a raise and want to know **how to calculate pay raise percentage** manually, you need two values: your **current (old) rate of pay** and your **new rate of pay**.

### The Pay Raise Percentage Formula
To find the percentage increase, you calculate the difference between the new pay and the old pay, divide that difference by the old pay, and then multiply by 100.

$$\text{Pay Raise \%} = \left(\frac{\text{New Pay} - \text{Old Pay}}{\text{Old Pay}}\right) \times 100$$

### Step-by-Step Example for Salaried Employees
Suppose you currently earn an annual salary of $72,000. During your annual review, your manager informs you that your salary will increase to $75,600 starting next month. Here is how you calculate the percentage of your raise:
1. **Find the raise amount (difference):** Subtract your old salary from your new salary.
   $$75,600 - 72,000 = 3,600$$
   Your annual raise amount is **$3,600**.
2. **Divide by the old salary:** Divide the raise amount by your original salary of $72,000.
   $$\frac{3,600}{72,000} = 0.05$$
3. **Convert to a percentage:** Multiply the result by 100.
   $$0.05 \times 100 = 5\%$$
   Your salary increased by exactly **5%**.

### Step-by-Step Example for Hourly Employees
This same formula applies to hourly wages. If you are an hourly worker earning $22.50 per hour, and your supervisor raises your wage to $24.30 per hour, you calculate the percentage as follows:
1. **Find the wage difference:** 
   $$24.30 - 22.50 = 1.80$$
   Your hourly increase is **$1.80 per hour**.
2. **Divide by the old wage:**
   $$\frac{1.80}{22.50} = 0.08$$
3. **Multiply by 100:**
   $$0.08 \times 100 = 8\%$$
   Your hourly pay rate increased by **8%**.

## How Do I Calculate My Pay Raise?

If you are thinking, "**how do i calculate my pay raise** when I only know the percentage or when my hours vary?" you need to account for your pay frequency and work hours.

### Converting an Hourly Raise to an Annual Salary
If you receive an hourly wage and want to know what a raise looks like on an annual basis, you must define your annual work hours. For a standard full-time employee working 40 hours per week for 52 weeks a year, the total hours worked per year is **2,080 hours** (40 hours × 52 weeks).

If you get a raise of **$2.00 per hour**, your annual raise is:
$$\text{Annual Raise} = \$2.00 \times 2,080 \text{ hours} = \$4,160 \text{ per year}$$

If you work part-time, say 25 hours per week, your annual hours are 1,300 (25 hours × 52 weeks). The same $2.00/hr raise equals:
$$\text{Annual Raise} = \$2.00 \times 1,300 \text{ hours} = \$2,600 \text{ per year}$$

### Applying a Percentage Raise to Your Pay Check
If you are given a 4.5% raise on an annual salary of $65,000, you compute the new figures by converting the percentage to a decimal (4.5% = 0.045) and multiplying:
1. **Calculate the raise amount:**
   $$\$65,000 \times 0.045 = \$2,925 \text{ annual raise}$$
2. **Calculate the new annual salary:**
   $$\$65,000 + \$2,925 = \$67,925 \text{ new annual salary}$$
3. **Determine the monthly difference:**
   $$\frac{\$2,925}{12} = \$243.75 \text{ extra per month (before taxes)}$$

## How Company Calculates Pay Raise Based on 5 Star Scale

Many modern corporate organizations do not give arbitrary raises. Instead, human resource departments use structured systems to determine merit-based salary increases. One of the most prevalent systems is a **5-star performance rating scale** mapped to a **compensation merit matrix**.

When examining **how company calculates pay raise based on 5 star scale**, two primary factors dictate your percentage:
1. **Your Performance Rating (1 to 5 Stars)**
2. **Your Compa-Ratio (Market Position)**

### 1. The Performance Rating Scale
At the end of a fiscal year, employees receive a score from 1 to 5 based on their performance reviews:
- **5 Stars (Outstanding / Far Exceeds Expectations):** Typically commands the highest raise pool, ranging from **7.0% to 10.0%**.
- **4 Stars (Exceeds Expectations):** Typically commands a strong raise, ranging from **5.0% to 6.5%**.
- **3 Stars (Meets Expectations / Satisfactory):** Represents the corporate average. It aligns with standard cost-of-living adjustments, ranging from **3.0% to 4.5%**.
- **2 Stars (Needs Improvement):** Below average performance. Yields minimal raises of **1.0% to 2.5%**, or sometimes 0%.
- **1 Star (Unsatisfactory):** Substandard performance. Results in **0% raise** and typically triggers a Performance Improvement Plan (PIP).

### 2. The Role of the Compa-Ratio
A **Compa-Ratio** measures your current salary against the market midpoint for your specific job grade. 

$$\text{Compa-Ratio} = \left(\frac{\text{Your Salary}}{\text{Market Midpoint Salary}}\right) \times 100$$

Companies use the Compa-Ratio to ensure internal equity and market alignment:
- **Low Compa-Ratio (Under 90%):** You are paid below the market average. To prevent you from leaving, HR will often apply a **0.5% to 1.0% merit boost** on top of your performance rating raise.
- **High Compa-Ratio (Over 110%):** You are already paid above the market average for your role. To control costs, the company may cap your raise, applying a **0.5% to 1.0% deduction** or converting the raise into a one-time cash bonus rather than adding it to your base salary.

### The Merit Matrix Calculation
Here is an example of a corporate merit matrix combining rating and market position:

| Performance Rating | Below Midpoint (<90% Compa) | At Midpoint (90%–110% Compa) | Above Midpoint (>110% Compa) |
|:-------------------|:----------------------------|:-----------------------------|:-----------------------------|
| **5 Stars**        | 9.5% – 10.0% Raise          | 8.0% – 9.0% Raise            | 7.0% – 7.5% Raise            |
| **4 Stars**        | 6.0% – 6.5% Raise          | 5.0% – 5.5% Raise            | 4.0% – 4.5% Raise            |
| **3 Stars**        | 4.0% – 4.5% Raise          | 3.0% – 3.5% Raise            | 2.0% – 2.5% Raise            |
| **2 Stars**        | 1.5% – 2.0% Raise          | 1.0% – 1.5% Raise            | 0.0% Raise                   |
| **1 Star**         | 0.0% Raise                  | 0.0% Raise                   | 0.0% Raise                   |

By utilizing this structured method, companies ensure that budget allocations are distributed equitably, rewarding top performers while maintaining market competitiveness.

## How to Calculate Back Pay for a Raise

In corporate and public sector environments, a raise might be approved months after the date it was scheduled to go into effect. In these scenarios, the employer owes the employee retrofitted compensation, commonly known as **back pay**.

If you need to know **how to calculate back pay for a raise**, you must calculate the difference between what you were actually paid and what you should have been paid over the backdated period.

### The Back Pay Formula for Hourly Employees
For hourly workers, back pay is calculated by finding your hourly rate difference and multiplying it by the total hours worked (both regular and overtime) during the period.

$$\text{Hourly Difference} = \text{New Hourly Rate} - \text{Old Hourly Rate}$$
$$\text{Gross Back Pay} = \text{Hourly Difference} \times \text{Hours Worked}$$

**Example:** 
Your wage increase from $25.00/hr to $27.50/hr was approved on June 1st, but backdated to April 1st. 
1. **Find the hourly difference:**
   $$\$27.50 - \$25.00 = \$2.50 \text{ per hour}$$
2. **Determine hours worked:** In those two months (8 weeks), you worked 40 hours per week.
   $$8 \text{ weeks} \times 40 \text{ hours} = 320 \text{ hours}$$
3. **Calculate gross back pay:**
   $$\$2.50 \times 320 \text{ hours} = \$800.00 \text{ gross back pay owed}$$

### The Back Pay Formula for Salaried Employees
For salaried employees, the process is similar but uses pay periods or monthly divisions:

$$\text{Monthly Difference} = \text{New Monthly Salary} - \text{Old Monthly Salary}$$
$$\text{Gross Back Pay} = \text{Monthly Difference} \times \text{Months Backdated}$$

**Example:**
Your annual salary was raised from $72,000 to $78,000. The raise is backdated by 3 months.
1. **Find monthly rates:**
   - Old monthly salary: $\$72,000 / 12 = \$6,000$
   - New monthly salary: $\$78,000 / 12 = \$6,500$
2. **Find the difference:**
   $$\$6,500 - \$6,000 = \$500 \text{ per month}$$
3. **Multiply by months backdated:**
   $$\$500 \times 3 \text{ months} = \$1,500.00 \text{ gross back pay owed}$$

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How do I calculate my pay raise percentage?
To calculate your pay raise percentage, subtract your original pay rate from your new pay rate. Then, divide that figure by your original pay rate and multiply the result by 100. For example, if your pay goes from $20/hr to $22/hr, you divide the $2 difference by the original $20 to get 0.10, which equates to a 10% raise.

### How do you calculate pay raise percentage for hourly employees?
For hourly employees, you calculate the pay raise percentage by using your hourly rates. Subtract your old hourly wage from your new hourly wage, divide the result by the old hourly wage, and multiply by 100. This isolates the calculation from the total hours worked, giving you the exact percentage boost in your base rate.

### How to calculate a pay raise percentage when moving from hourly to salaried?
To calculate a pay raise percentage when transitioning from an hourly wage to an annual salary, you must first convert your hourly rate to its annual equivalent. Multiply your hourly rate by your annual hours (typically 2,080 for a full-time 40-hour work week). Once you have both rates in annual terms, apply the standard percentage formula: subtract the old annual equivalent from the new salary, divide by the old equivalent, and multiply by 100.
